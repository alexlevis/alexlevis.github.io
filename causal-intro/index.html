Causality, counterfactuals, confounding

Alex Levis
 2022-08-11  508 words   3 minutes 
Contents
  • Counterfactuals and causality

In introductory statistics courses, one often encounters the phrase: “correlation does not imply causation.” As a statistician who studies causality, I would amend this phrase to say: “correlation does not necessarily imply causation, but it can.” In this post, I introduce a framework for bridging the worlds of statistics and causality, and talk about when and how we can quantify so-called causal effects from data observed in the real world.

Disclaimer 1: these ideas, of course, are not mine. There are countless works over the past century in which statistical causal inference has been formalized and developed, but I make no attempt to give an exhaustive/fair list of references as I might were I writing in the traditional academic style. I simply aim to give an overview of the core ideas as I currently understand them, and point to references sparingly.

Disclaimer 2: as such a foundational scientific concept, there are quite a few useful models of causality that persist in various academic subcircles. I have grown up in the potential outcome and non-parametric structural equation model circles, and am partial to the ideas used by Jamie Robins and his academic descendants. Therefore, in these posts, I use notation and language in line with that standard. The book What If by Hernan & Robins is an excellent reference.

Counterfactuals and causality

In causal inference, we are often interested in quantifying the effect of an exposure/treatment, call it \(A\), on an outcome interest, call it \(Y\). The concept of potential outcomes or counterfactuals is highly effective for this purpose: for each possible fixed exposure level \(a\), we introduce a variable \(Y(a)\) to denote the value that the outcome would take in a world where we intervened and forced the exposure level to take the value \(A = a\). Of course, in the real world, the (f)actual exposure level \(A\) is potentially determined by a complex interplay of factors. But we can imagine a world where such an intervention occurs, and thus can conceptualize treatment effects in this light. When exposure is binary, say \(A\) takes the value \(0\) or \(1\), the effect of exposure for a given subject is given by contrasting \(Y(1)\) and \(Y(0)\), on some scale. For instance, the additive individual treatment effect is equal to \(Y(1) - Y(0)\), i.e., the difference in the outcome for a given subject had their exposure been set to \(A = 1\), versus if it had been set to \(A = 0\). Such a contrast is “causal” in that this difference reflects the downstream effects of a change in exposure level alone, i.e., no other factor is intervened upon.

The fundamental problem of causal inference is that we only ever observe one level of treatment on a given subject. Under the (often reasonable) consistency assumption, the observed or factual outcome \(Y\) is equal to \(Y(A)\).
